Student Stress Level Predictions

Objectives

  • Address the rising mental health challenges experienced by students in academic settings
  • Identify key stress-driving factors across psychological, social, and academic domains
  • Understand how various lifestyle habits, academic pressures, and interpersonal dynamics influence stress
  • Help educators and institutions enhance curricula and support systems based on data-driven findings
  • Provide actionable insights to guide the development of stress-management interventions and wellness programs
  • Raise awareness among policymakers and school administrators about the urgency of student mental health

    • Methodology

  • Utilized a Kaggle dataset with 1100 entries and 21 features covering psychological, physiological, environmental, academic, and social factors
  • Performed exploratory data analysis (EDA) to validate data quality — no missing values or outliers were found
  • Used correlation analysis and Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) to identify key variables and address multicollinearity
  • Split data into 80% training and 20% testing sets, and used 10-fold cross-validation for reliable model evaluation
  • Trained Decision Tree, Naïve Bayes, and SVM models to classify stress levels
  • Tested four modeling strategies: no preprocessing, PCA for feature reduction, removal of correlated variables, and parameter tuning via Grid Search
  • Selected the best model based on test accuracy and generalization capability
  • Identified depression, academic performance, and self-esteem as the top contributors to student stress

    • Data Overview

    Data Source
    • Kaggle: Stress Factors Dataset

    This dataset provides insights into student stress levels based on psychological, physiological, academic, social, and environmental factors. It includes 1,100 records and 21 features, with no missing values or outliers. Ideal for classification and correlation analysis, it supports exploring what most impacts student stress.

    Key Data Points

    Product name, brand name, price (in USD), skin type, category classifications, user reviews, and ratings

    Data Pre-processing

  • Missing Data Check: Performed a column-wise scan for missing values; confirmed that the dataset was fully complete with no missing entries.
  • Outlier Detection: Applied the z-score method to all numeric features using a 3-standard-deviation threshold.
  • Result: No outliers were detected, indicating all data points fall within a normal range and are suitable for modeling.
  • Conclusion: The dataset required no removal or imputation, ensuring a clean and reliable foundation for further analysis.

    Important Features
  • Academic Performance: Negatively correlated with stress levels, indicating that poor performance increases stress.
  • Depression: Strongly positively correlated, showing a direct link to higher stress levels.
  • Self-Esteem: Negatively correlated, where lower self-esteem leads to greater stress.

    • Violin Plot – Density Measures

    These violin plots illustrate how the distributions of three key features—academic performance, depression, and self-esteem—vary across different stress levels (low = 0, medium = 1, high = 2).

  • Academic performance tends to decrease as stress level increases, with lower values concentrated in higher stress categories.
  • Depression scores rise with stress level, showing a clear upward shift in the density distribution.
  • Self-esteem drops significantly as stress level increases, indicating an inverse relationship with stress.

    • These patterns confirm that these three features are highly predictive of student stress and align with earlier correlation and feature importance analyses.
