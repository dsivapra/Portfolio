TSA Baggage Claims Statistical Analysis

Objectives

  • Investigate if baggage claim rates significantly differ across major U.S. airports.
  • Analyze whether claim rates vary when broken down by both airport and quarter.
  • Assess if specific airlines show a higher frequency of claims, indicating variability in baggage handling practices.
  • Motivated by findings such as TSA paying over $3 million for 50,000 claims (USA TODAY), with JFK (857 claims) and LAX (791 claims) topping the list of busiest airports involved in settlements.
  • Support data-driven improvements in baggage handling and passenger experience by identifying patterns in TSA claim distributions.

Methodology

  • Obtained TSA claims data and U.S. passenger traffic data from Kaggle and other open sources.
  • Defined mainstream airports (DFW, JFK, LAX, LGA, ORD, SFO), four major airlines (American, Delta, United, Southwest), and focused analysis on the year 2015.
  • Calculated the number of claims by airport and airline, and total passengers by airport and quarter.
  • Used Chi-square tests to evaluate claim rate differences across:
    • Airports
    • Airports by quarter
    • Airlines (with airline passenger volume estimated using market share data)
  • Adopted a significance level of Î± = 0.05 to determine statistical relevance in all tests.

Data Overview

Data Sources
  • TSA Claims Database
  • Passenger Count Dataset

Two datasets from Kaggle were used to conduct this analysis. The TSA Claims Database includes property and injury claims filed from 2002 to 2015, providing details like incident date, airport, airline, and claim type. It helped calculate the number of claims filed per airline and airport. The second dataset, US Airline Flight Routes and Fares, spans from 1993 to 2024 and offers insights into passenger volumes by airport and quarter. This allowed us to normalize claim counts by passenger traffic, offering a fair comparison of claim rates across locations and carriers.

Statistical Analysis: Part 1

Chi-square test was conducted to determine if there was a difference in the proportion of claims across major airports.

Target Scope:

  • Airports: DFW, JFK, LAX, LGA, ORD, SFO (6 airports)
  • Airlines: American Airlines, Delta Airlines, Southwest Airlines, United Airlines (4 airlines)
  • Year: 2015

Hypothesis Test:

H0: The proportion of the claims is the same among airports

Conclusion: Reject H0

  • The data shows that there are statistically significant differences in the proportion of claims among airports.

Statistical Analysis: Part 2

Chi-square test was conducted to determine if there was a difference in the proportion of claims by airport and season (quarter).

H0: the proportion of the claim are the same among airports and seasons

Conclusion:

  • For LAX, JFK, DFW: Reject H0
  • For SFO, ORD, LGA: Do not reject H0

Statistical Analysis: Part 3

Chi-square test was conducted to determine if there was a difference in claim proportions by airlines.

H0: the proportion of the claim are the same among airlines

  • Need: The total number of passengers at each airline

Issue:
Dataset does not provide the total number of passengers for each airline, only totals across all airlines.

Solution:
Use airline market share to estimate the total number of passengers for each airline.

Conclusion: Reject H0

  • The data shows that there are differences in the proportion of claims among airlines.

Limitations

  • The dataset does not account for flight frequency or airport capacity, which can influence claim volume.
  • Lack of individual airline passenger data makes it harder to assess per-airline claim rates accurately.
  • Analysis is based on data from a single year â 2015 â which limits the ability to track trends over time.
  • Only domestic flight data was included, excluding international operations where claim behavior may differ.
  • No details were provided on baggage handling procedures, which could directly impact claim incidents.
  • The market share used to estimate passengers by airline may not perfectly reflect actual travel volumes for 2015.
  • Passenger demographics or travel purpose, which could affect claim likelihood, were not available.
